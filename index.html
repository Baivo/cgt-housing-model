Overview

This model estimates the impact of changes to Australia's capital gains tax (CGT) discount on housing prices, first home buyer affordability, and government revenue. It uses piecewise linear interpolation between published estimates from multiple independent Australian research institutions, with confidence bands showing the range of estimates.

Confidence Bands

All price impact estimates are presented with three scenarios reflecting the range of published research:

ScenarioBasisScaling

This approach transparently shows users the uncertainty in the estimates rather than presenting a single point estimate. The true impact likely falls within this range.

CGT Discount Mechanism

The CGT discount allows individuals and trusts to reduce their taxable capital gain by 50% for assets held longer than 12 months. Introduced on 21 September 1999, it replaced the previous cost-base indexation method. The effective tax rate on capital gains is: effective_rate = marginal_rate × (1 - discount%).

Non-Linear CGT × Negative Gearing Interaction

v2 improvement: The model now uses a multiplicative interaction between CGT and negative gearing, replacing the v1 additive approximation. The reasoning: when the CGT discount is high (50%), investors rely heavily on negative gearing to fund holding costs while waiting for tax-advantaged capital gains. Removing NG at full CGT discount has maximum effect. When CGT discount is already reduced, capital gains strategies are less attractive, so removing NG has a smaller incremental effect.

Formula: NG_effect = base_NG_effect × (0.5 + 0.5 × CGT_discount / 50)

Stamp Duty Calculation

v2 addition: State-specific stamp duty is now calculated using simplified graduated rate schedules from each state's revenue office. First home buyer exemptions and concessions are applied where applicable. This shows users the total upfront cost (deposit + stamp duty) rather than deposit alone, giving a more realistic picture of barriers to entry.

Interest Rate Sensitivity

v2 addition: Users can now vary the mortgage interest rate (3.5% to 8.5%) to see how rate changes interact with price changes. Monthly mortgage payments and income needed for serviceability (at 30% of income) update in real time. The RBA cash rate is 3.85% as of February 2026, with typical variable mortgage rates around 6.0-6.5%.

Supply-Side Impact

v2 addition: The model now estimates the effect on new housing construction and rents, calibrated to the Grattan Institute's estimate of approximately 10,000 fewer homes over 5 years (2,000/year) at a 25% discount level, with a rent impact of less than $1 per week. These are scaled linearly for other discount levels. The Grattan Institute notes this supply impact could be more than offset if even a small portion of the reform revenue were directed to social housing or planning reform.

Phase-In Timeline

v2 addition: Rather than showing only the steady-state outcome, the model now includes a 5-year phase-in timeline based on the Grattan Institute's recommendation of gradual implementation rather than grandfathering. This shows how price adjustments and FHB share changes would unfold over time, giving a more realistic picture of the transition.

Key Assumptions

  • Typical investor marginal tax rate: 39%
  • Average holding period: 7 years
  • Average annual capital growth: 6.5%
  • Deposit percentage: 20%
  • Annual household savings rate: $40,000
  • Median household income: $105,000
  • Default mortgage rate: 6.0% (adjustable via slider)
  • Loan term: 30 years
  • Serviceability threshold: 30% of gross income

Remaining Limitations

  • Uses piecewise linear interpolation calibrated to published estimates, not a full general equilibrium model
  • Supply-side estimates are scaled linearly from a single calibration point (Grattan, 25% discount); actual supply responses may be non-linear
  • Stamp duty schedules are simplified approximations; actual duty may vary based on property type, eligibility, and concession changes
  • Does not model state-level land tax or planning regulation differences beyond stamp duty
  • Does not model migration patterns, foreign investment, or population growth effects
  • City-level investor shares are estimates derived from ABS lending patterns (confidence: moderate)
  • Phase-in timeline assumes linear adjustment; actual market adjustment may be front-loaded or delayed
