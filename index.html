Overview

This model estimates the impact of changes to Australia's capital gains tax (CGT) discount on housing prices, first home buyer affordability, and government revenue. It uses piecewise linear interpolation between published estimates from multiple independent Australian research institutions.

The model is not a general equilibrium model. It provides indicative estimates based on the best available published research, intended to illustrate the direction and approximate magnitude of policy impacts. Users should treat the outputs as estimates within a range of uncertainty.

CGT Discount Mechanism

The CGT discount allows individuals and trusts to reduce their taxable capital gain by 50% for assets held longer than 12 months. Introduced on 21 September 1999, it replaced the previous cost-base indexation method. The effective tax rate on capital gains is: effective_rate = marginal_rate × (1 - discount%). For a typical investor at the 39% marginal rate, the current effective CGT rate is approximately 19.5%.

The model interpolates housing price impacts between these published estimates:

Treasury modelling found that combined reform of CGT and negative gearing would reduce house prices by a maximum of 4.5%. The Grattan Institute estimated that reducing the discount to 25% alone would reduce prices by less than 1%. These estimates are consistent and form a credible range for interpolation.

City-Level Adjustment

National price impacts are adjusted for each city using an investor concentration multiplier. Cities with a higher share of investor lending relative to the national average are assumed to be more sensitive to CGT changes. The multiplier is calculated as: multiplier = city_investor_share / national_investor_share. For example, Sydney (NSW) has approximately 43% investor share versus the 40.4% national average, giving a multiplier of approximately 1.06.

Negative Gearing Interaction

When negative gearing is toggled off, an additional price reduction of approximately 1.3% is applied (derived from Treasury's combined estimate of -4.5% minus the CGT-only estimate of -3.2%). Additional revenue of approximately $5.0B is added, and the FHB market share impact increases by 2.2 percentage points. This is a simplified additive approximation; in reality, CGT and negative gearing interact non-linearly.

First Home Buyer Metrics

  • Deposit required: 20% of the modelled dwelling price
  • Years to save: Deposit divided by assumed annual household savings of $40,000
  • Market share change: Calibrated to Warlters (2022) finding of +4.7pp owner-occupied share increase from halving the CGT discount and removing negative gearing, with approximately 2.5pp attributed to CGT alone. This gives a rate of approximately 0.1pp per 1pp reduction in the CGT discount.

Revenue Impact

Revenue estimates are interpolated between the Grattan Institute (+$6.5B/year at 25% discount) and e61 Institute (+$2.85B/year at 33% discount). The current CGT discount costs the federal budget an estimated $19.7 billion annually (Treasury Tax Expenditures and Insights Statement 2024-25).

Key Assumptions

  • Typical investor marginal tax rate: 39%
  • Average holding period: 7 years
  • Average annual capital growth: 6.5%
  • Deposit percentage: 20%
  • Annual household savings rate: $40,000
  • Median household income: $105,000
  • Mortgage interest rate: 6.0% (for serviceability calculations)
  • Loan term: 30 years

Limitations

  • Uses simplified linear interpolation between published estimates, not a full general equilibrium model
  • CGT and negative gearing interact non-linearly; the additive approximation is a simplification
  • Published estimates vary significantly between institutions (Grattan: <1%, Treasury: up to 4.5%)
  • Does not account for supply-side responses (e.g. changes to housing construction)
  • Does not account for state-level stamp duty, land tax, or planning regulations
  • Does not model macroeconomic conditions, interest rate changes, or migration patterns
  • City-level investor shares are estimates derived from ABS lending patterns, not exact figures
  • Data points span different years (2022-23 tax stats, 2025 lending/price data) and may not perfectly align
  • The model assumes static behaviour; in practice, policy changes would trigger dynamic market responses over time
